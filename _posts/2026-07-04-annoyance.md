I don't go looking for ideas. Something annoys me enough first, and I can't leave it alone.

---

## Annoyance #1: The docs only told half the story

Early on, I was asked to investigate whether a service could call another service directly.

The documentation only ever described the backend-to-backend case. There was no evidence anywhere that the piece I needed existed at all — so I concluded it didn't, and the team moved forward on that basis. 

By the time I caught it, we'd already built around that assumption — a problem better documentation would have made obvious from the start.

That annoyance is part of why I care so much about onboarding, ramp-time, and internal docs.

---

## Annoyance #2: Everyone was solving the same problem, alone

Every engineer I know is using AI differently — different prompts, different context, different rules files, all reinvented from scratch.

Nobody was coordinating any of it. I watched the same codebase context get re-explained to the same tool, over and over, one engineer at a time — for months, before anyone thought to write it down once.

That annoyance is why I think about AI adoption as an organizational workflow problem, not just a tooling one.

---

## Annoyance #3: Review didn't scale with generation

AI made it easy to produce a lot of code fast. It didn't make it any easier to verify that code is actually right — there was just more of it to check.

That annoyance turned into a couple of small tools — one that flags which parts of a fresh diff actually deserve a closer look, and one that helps write clearer context for whoever reviews the change next. Different shapes, same underlying question: *out of everything AI just generated, what actually deserves attention first?*

---

## The pattern

I don't go looking for ideas. I go looking for the thing that's bothering me, and I ask why it's still like this.

Most of the time, the answer is just this — nobody's gotten around to it yet. But, working in that gap is exactly where I want to be.
